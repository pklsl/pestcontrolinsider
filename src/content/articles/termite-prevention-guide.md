Termites are responsible for more damage to American homes than fires and storms combined. But unlike most natural disasters, termite damage is almost entirely preventable. With the right combination of structural modifications, moisture control, and professional prevention treatments, you can protect your home from one of its most costly threats.

This guide covers everything you need to do to prevent termites — organized from highest to lowest impact.

## Understand Your Risk

Termite risk varies significantly by region. The highest-risk areas in the U.S.:
- The entire Southeast (Florida, Georgia, Alabama, Mississippi, Louisiana, South Carolina, North Carolina)
- Gulf Coast states
- Southern California (drywood termites)
- The Mid-Atlantic region
- Texas and Oklahoma

If you live in a high-risk state, prevention isn't optional — it's essential maintenance. If you live in the northern U.S., risk is lower but not zero.

## Tier 1: Structural Modifications (Highest Impact)

### Eliminate Wood-to-Soil Contact

This is the single most important termite prevention measure. Subterranean termites travel from soil to wood — eliminating direct contact cuts their access.

**Action items:**
- Ensure all wood siding, door frames, and structural wood is at least 6 inches above soil grade
- Replace wood posts in contact with the ground with concrete footings
- Remove wood form boards buried against the foundation from previous construction
- Ensure wood decks don't have posts buried directly in soil — use adjustable post bases

### Eliminate Wood Debris Near the Foundation

Termites establish in wood debris before moving to the structure.

- Remove all wood debris, lumber scraps, and construction material from around and under the home
- Remove dead tree stumps within 20 feet of the structure
- Remove old wood fencing in contact with the soil near the foundation
- Elevate firewood at least 12 inches off the ground and store at least 20 feet from the house

### Reduce Mulch Against the Foundation

Mulch retains moisture and provides both food and habitat for termites. Maintain an 18-inch bare zone around the foundation, or switch to inorganic mulch (gravel, rubber mulch) for the area immediately against the foundation.

### Seal Cracks in the Foundation

Subterranean termites exploit cracks in concrete foundations as entry points (even though they can't eat concrete). Seal cracks with hydraulic cement or epoxy injection, particularly around:
- Expansion joints in the slab
- Utility penetrations (plumbing, electrical)
- Cracks in foundation walls

## Tier 2: Moisture Control (Very High Impact)

Termites are strongly attracted to moisture — both for survival and because moist wood is easier to eat. Eliminating excess moisture near the structure dramatically reduces its attractiveness.

### Fix All Plumbing Leaks

Any leaking pipe — interior or exterior — creates the moisture conditions termites prefer. Address:
- Crawl space moisture from condensation or ground moisture
- Roof leaks that wet wood framing
- Foundation leaks creating wet basement conditions
- Irrigation systems that wet the foundation

### Improve Crawl Space Ventilation

A damp, poorly ventilated crawl space is among the highest-risk environments for subterranean termites.

- Ensure crawl space vents are open, unobstructed, and intact
- Install a vapor barrier (6-mil polyethylene) across the crawl space floor
- Consider a crawl space encapsulation system for high-moisture situations
- Install a crawl space dehumidifier if humidity remains high despite ventilation

### Grade Soil Away from the Foundation

Soil that slopes toward the foundation traps rainwater against the structure. Grade the soil at a slope of at least 6 inches of fall over the first 10 feet from the foundation.

### Maintain Gutters and Downspouts

Clogged gutters overflow and saturate soil against the foundation. Extend downspouts at least 6 feet from the foundation to direct water away.

### Fix Roof Leaks Promptly

Drywood termites often enter through gaps in roof materials. Any roof leak that wets wood framing provides both moisture and access.

## Tier 3: Professional Termite Prevention Treatments

For homes in moderate to high-risk areas, structural modifications and moisture control alone may not be sufficient. Professional prevention treatments add a chemical barrier.

### Liquid Termiticide Soil Treatment (Termidor)

A preventive soil treatment applies termiticide around the foundation perimeter before termites arrive. This is the most effective chemical prevention method.

**Termidor SC** (fipronil) applied by a licensed professional creates a 10+ year treated zone in the soil that kills termites before they reach the structure. It's the same product used for active infestations — applied preventively, it's far less expensive than treating an established colony.

Typical cost: $400–$1,500 for a preventive application, depending on foundation perimeter.

### Termite Monitoring and Bait Systems

In-ground bait stations installed around the perimeter intercept foraging termites before they reach the structure. Workers that find the bait stations consume the bait and transfer it back to the colony.

**Sentricon Always Active** and **Advance Termite Bait System** are the leading professional bait products. Both require installation and ongoing monitoring by licensed technicians.

A bait monitoring system provides:
- Early detection of termite activity
- Ongoing population suppression
- Annual monitoring visits that catch any new activity

Typical cost: $1,200–$2,500 initial installation + $300–$600/year for monitoring.

### DIY Bait Stations

**Spectracide Terminate Bait Sticks** are a consumer-available bait option that can supplement professional treatments. While significantly less effective than professional systems, they provide some monitoring capability and modest prevention in low-risk areas.

Place stations every 10 feet around the foundation perimeter. Check monthly and replace bait when consumed.

### Borate Wood Treatment

Borate salts (sodium borate, disodium octaborate tetrahydrate) penetrate wood and create a long-lasting toxic zone that kills termites and other wood-destroying organisms that consume treated wood.

**Tim-bor** and **Boracare** are professional-grade borate treatments applied to exposed wood in crawl spaces and during construction. They're highly effective for pre-treatment of new construction and for treating exposed structural wood in crawl spaces and attics.

## Tier 4: Inspection and Monitoring

The best prevention includes regular inspection to catch any activity early — before extensive damage occurs.

### Annual Professional Inspections

In moderate to high-risk areas, an annual termite inspection by a licensed pest control company is considered standard practice. Many states require a termite clearance letter (WDI report) for real estate transactions.

The inspection includes:
- Full interior and exterior inspection
- Crawl space and basement inspection
- Documentation of any findings
- Recommendations for treatment or prevention

Cost: $75–$150 for a standard inspection; often free with a service contract.

### Self-Inspection Checklist

Between professional inspections, conduct your own quarterly walkthrough:
- [ ] Inspect foundation perimeter for mud tubes
- [ ] Check crawl space for moisture, wood debris, and mud tubes
- [ ] Tap wood around door frames and windows — listen for hollow sound
- [ ] Check for swarmers or discarded wings in spring
- [ ] Inspect exterior wood (decks, fences) for soft spots or damage
- [ ] Confirm mulch is pulled back from the foundation

## Prevention Checklist: Complete Summary

**Structural:**
- [ ] Maintain 6-inch clearance between wood and soil
- [ ] Remove wood debris and stumps from near the foundation
- [ ] Pull mulch 18 inches from the foundation
- [ ] Seal foundation cracks
- [ ] Store firewood off-ground, 20 feet from house

**Moisture:**
- [ ] Fix all plumbing leaks promptly
- [ ] Install crawl space vapor barrier
- [ ] Grade soil away from foundation
- [ ] Maintain gutters and extend downspouts
- [ ] Ventilate or encapsulate crawl space

**Professional treatments (recommended in high-risk areas):**
- [ ] Apply preventive soil treatment (Termidor)
- [ ] Install monitoring bait system
- [ ] Treat crawl space wood with borate
- [ ] Schedule annual inspection

## Bottom Line

Termite prevention is most cost-effective when it starts before termites arrive. For homes in high-risk areas, the combination of structural modifications, moisture control, and a professional soil treatment or bait monitoring system provides reliable long-term protection. For the rest of the country, eliminating wood-to-soil contact, controlling moisture, and scheduling annual inspections provides meaningful risk reduction at low cost. The investment in prevention is always a fraction of the cost of structural termite repair.
