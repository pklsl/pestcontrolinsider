A mattress encasement is the single most cost-effective bed bug product you can buy — whether you're dealing with an active infestation or just protecting a new mattress. The right encasement traps bed bugs inside the mattress (or box spring) where they eventually die, and it prevents new bugs from establishing inside.

But not all encasements are equal. Cheap covers have zipper teeth gaps too wide to prevent eggs from passing through, or fabrics that bed bugs can bite through. Here's how to choose — and our top picks for 2026.

## What to Look for in a Bed Bug Encasement

### 1. Bite-Proof Fabric
The fabric must be woven tightly enough that bed bug mouthparts cannot pierce it. Look for encasements that are **lab-tested and certified** for bed bug bite resistance. The standard is fabric that passes ASTM bite-proof certification testing.

### 2. Zipper Quality and Closure
The zipper is the most common failure point. Quality encasements use:
- Zippers with fine teeth that block even eggs (1mm or smaller spacing)
- A velcro flap or cinch cover over the zipper — bed bugs can crawl along zipper teeth and find microscopic gaps
- Anti-snag zipper pulls

### 3. Depth
An encasement that doesn't fully cover a thick mattress will pull and stress the fabric, creating failure points. Measure your mattress depth (including any topper) and choose an encasement rated for that depth plus 2 inches.

### 4. Waterproofing
Not essential for bed bug protection alone, but a waterproof membrane adds protection against spills, sweat, and allergens. Look for noiseless waterproof membranes — some crinkle loudly and disrupt sleep.

### 5. Durability and Warranty
A quality encasement should last at least 2 years with regular use. Look for reinforced seams and a manufacturer warranty against zipper and fabric failure.

## How to Install a Mattress Encasement

1. Remove all bedding and launder on hot
2. With a helper, place the mattress inside the encasement — stand it on its side if needed
3. Zip closed completely — run your finger along the entire zipper to confirm no gaps
4. Close the Velcro flap over the zipper pull (if included)
5. Reinstall on the bed frame
6. Repeat for the box spring
7. Install ClimbUp Interceptors under each leg of the bed frame

## How Long Does an Encasement Last?

A quality encasement should last 2–5 years with normal use. Check annually for:
- Zipper integrity — run the zipper and check for teeth gaps
- Fabric tears or thinning areas along seams
- Waterproof layer integrity (if applicable)

## How Long Do Bed Bugs Survive Inside an Encasement?

Bed bugs trapped inside an encasement will eventually die — but it takes time. Bed bugs can survive 12–18 months without feeding in some conditions. **Do not remove the encasement for at least 18 months** after installing it as part of a treatment program.

## When to Use an Encasement

- **During active treatment:** Install at the start of any bed bug treatment to prevent bugs escaping from the mattress
- **After treatment:** Leave on permanently as protection and monitoring
- **New mattress protection:** Any new mattress should be encased immediately — it's far easier to prevent than to treat
- **Travel:** Consider a travel encasement or pack the mattress immediately after hotel stays if you suspect exposure
